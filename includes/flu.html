Flu Relief with Natural Black Elderberry

We all know the knock-you-off-your-feet misery of the flu: the aches,
chills, fever, dry cough and lingering exhaustion. And chances are many
of us will feel it again sometime soon. In fact, experts at the US
centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimate that up to
one-fourth of the country's populations gets the flu in any one flu
season, which ordinarily runs from November to March. Children are two
to three times more likely than adults to get the flu. And kids often
spread the illness to adults. Most people who come down with he flu
recover within a week. But influenza and its complications, including
bronchitis and pneumonia, are serious enough to cause more then 100,000
hospitalization and up to 70,000 deaths each year in the United States
alone.

For whom is the risk of complications most significant?

* Newborns
* Anyone over age 50
* People with chronic illnesses, including heart disease or diabetes

These individuals are also at the greatest risk of experiencing life -
threatening complications with influenza

What Causes Flu?

Behind the symptoms of flu is a respiratory infection caused by any one
of a variety of influenza viruses that scientists have separated into
types A, B, and C. Type A is the most common and can lead to serious flu
epidemics. Type B can also causes epidemics, but its symptoms are
typically much milder To date, no major epidemics have been linked to
Type C viruses.

We catch the flu by being around someone who already has the illness, so
it's not surprising that we are most in danger of becoming infected in
areas crowded with people, such as theaters, schools, and airplanes.
When people with he flu cough or sneeze the virus is passed into the air
via respiratory droplets witch we can then inhale. We can also become
infected by touching objects that a person with the flu has
touched(telephones, doorknobs, and pencils) and then transferring the
virus to our nose or mouth. For children the close quarters they keep
with classmates at school increases their potential for infection and
the subsequent spread of the flu

Prevention and Vaccination

How can flu be prevented? Prevention or those people most at risk beings
with immunization against influenza. Flu shots are usually effective,
especially when received six to eight weeks before flu season beings.
Each year a new vaccine is made based on scientific evaluation of the
flu viruses currently circulation or those expected to circulate later
that winter. Sometimes, however, and unexpected flu virus appears, for
with the vaccine may not be as effective. Universal precautions included
limiting our interaction with those who already have the flu and washing
our hands after coming in contact with them or any objects they may have
touched.
